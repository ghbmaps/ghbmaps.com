What is a graticule?

A graticule is the network of lines that shows the Earth’s parallels of latitude and meridians of longitude. It provides a way to express location in geographic coordinates (degrees of latitude and longitude).

Latitude & Longitude

The latitude/longitude grid is based on a sphere and is familiar to most map readers: lines of latitude run east–west and stay parallel to the equator; lines of longitude run north–south and meet at the poles. On most maps, latitude lines appear horizontal and longitude lines vertical.

Latitude

  • Lines parallel to the equator
  • Hemispheres designated North (N) and South (S).
  • Equator is 0°
  • North Pole is N 90°
  • South Pole is S 90°
  • Expressed in three formats
    • Degrees, Minutes, Seconds N 44° 09′ 39.03″
    • Degrees, Minutes N 44° 09.65′
    • Degrees N 44.160°
  • 1° of latitude is 111.12 km (69.05 mi.)
  • 1′ of latitude is one nautical mile (1.85 km, 1.15 mi.)
  • 1″ of latitude is 30.8 m (101 feet)

Longitude

  • Lines run from pole to pole
  • Greenwich, England is the Prime Meridian.
  • Hemispheres designated West (W) and East (E) of the Prime Meridian
  • Prime Meridian is 0°
  • Expressed in three formats
    • Degrees, Minutes, Seconds W 71° 38′ 39.95″
    • Degrees, Minutes W 71° 38.6659′
    • Degrees W 71.644°
  • 1° of longitude is 111.12 km (69.05 mi.) only at the equator
  • 1′ of longitude is 1 nautical mile only at the equator

A typical geographic coordinate is written as latitude, longitude, e.g., N44° 09.65′, W71° 38.6659′.

Graticules are used to show the location in geographic coordinates (degrees of latitude and longitude). Latitude and longitude coordinates are usually written with hemisphere latitude, hemisphere longitude (N44° 09.65′, W71° 38.6659′) and referred to as a geographic coordinate because it is based on a sphere. WGS 1984 UTM Zone 19N (Meters)

The UTM grid divides the Earth between 84° N and 80° S into 60 zones of 6° longitude. Zones are numbered from 1 at 180° W eastward to 60 at 180° E, and each zone is split into northern and southern hemispheres. For example, UTM Zone 19N covers 72° W to 66° W in the northern hemisphere. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Eastings increase eastward from a false origin 500,000 m west of the zone’s central meridian; northings increase northward from the equator (in the northern hemisphere). On hiking maps, grid tick marks often appear every 500 m, making it easy to estimate partial distances from labeled km lines.

Below is a description of how the UTM grid values are referenced and formatted in WGS 1984 UTM Zone 19N .

Eastings

  • Increasing easting numbers means you are going east.
  • Full easting coordinate number 288000E represents 288,000 meters east of the false origin.
  • The false origin is 500,000 meters west of the UTM zone’s central meridian (−69°W).
  • Distance between 288000E and 290000E is 2000 meters (2 km); each tic mark represents 500 meters.
  • The last 3 numbers stand for meters.
  • Hiking maps shown above include corner labels, e.g., 290,994E m.
  • Corner labels can be read as “two hundred ninety thousand, nine hundred ninety-four meters east.”

Northings

  • Increasing northing numbers means you are going north.
  • Full northing coordinate 4892000N represents 4,892,000 meters north of the equator.
  • Distance between 4892000N and 4894000N is 2000 meters (2 km); each tic mark represents 500 meters.
  • The last 3 numbers stand for meters.
  • Hiking maps shown above include corner labels, e.g., 4,894,875N m.
  • Corner labels can be read as “four million, eight hundred ninety-four thousand, eight hundred seventy-five meters north.”

The standard meridians are 180 kilometers to the east and west of the central meridian. Each UTM zone has its own projection and is designed to maintain accuracy of at least one part in 2500 (i.e., distance measured over a 2500-meter course of the UTM grid system would be accurate within a meter). In the northern hemisphere, UTM coordinates are measured from a false origin located at the equator and 500,000 meters west of the UTM zone’s central meridian.

In the southern hemisphere, UTM coordinates are measured from a false origin located at 10,000,000 meters south of the equator and 500,000 meters west of the UTM zone’s central meridian. A datum is part of the definition of a projected coordinate system; the UTM grid system may be based on NAD27, NAD83, or WGS84. For example, if UTM Zone 19N is based on WGS84, then its full designation reads WGS 1984 UTM Zone 19N. Take a few moments and explore the UTM Zone 19 displayed in a projected and geographic coordinate system.

Measured Grids

A measured grid is a pattern of lines drawn on a map that uniquely locates any point using projected coordinates—often a combination of numbers (and sometimes letters). Many GPS receivers can display those same projected coordinates, so the numbers on the screen match the grid on your map. Be sure your device supports the same grid and units as your paper or web maps.

Two common examples you’ll encounter in New England mapping projects are the NAD 1983 Contiguous USA Albers grid and the NAD 1983 State Plane FIPS 2800 grid for New Hampshire.

NAD 1983 State Plane New Hampshire FIPS 2800 (Feet)

The State Plane Coordinate (SPC) system was developed in the 1930s to record survey monuments with very high local accuracy—maximum scale distortion of roughly 1 part in 10,000 (about four times tighter than UTM’s typical 1:2,500 spec). SPC is a grid laid over a projection tailored to a state or sub-state area, and is widely used in engineering, public works, and property mapping. Some consumer GPS devices and apps can report coordinates directly in SPC.
