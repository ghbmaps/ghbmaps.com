Horizontal Coordinate Systems

There are two kinds of horizontal coordinate systems; a Geographic Coordinate System (GCS) and a Projected Coordinate System (PCS). A GCS is based on the globe and uses latitude and longitude—usually in degrees, though other formats exist. Latitude is measured north–south from the equator, and longitude is measured from an arbitrary zero line called the prime meridian. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Example of a Geographic Coordinate System

A common GCS is WGS 84. In Esri software, coordinate systems can be specified by a Well-Known ID (WKID) or Well-Known Text (WKT). WKIDs < 32767 are assigned by EPSG; those > 32767 are assigned by Esri. In the 19th century, Nicolas Auguste Tissot introduced a method (Tissot’s indicatrix) to compare distortion across map projections. Projected (Planar) Coordinate Systems & State Plane

A projection converts round-earth coordinates (degrees / angular measures) to a flat map with linear x,y units. New Hampshire uses the State Plane Coordinate System (SPCS)—a Cartesian grid over a map projection surface covering only the state. SPCS may use Transverse Mercator, Lambert Conformal Conic, or Hotine Oblique Mercator. New Hampshire uses Transverse Mercator and the PCS “NAD 1983 State Plane New Hampshire FIPS 2800 Feet” (WKID 3437, previously 102710). Vertical Coordinate Systems (VCS)

A Vertical Coordinate System has either a datum (measured from the ellipsoid) or a vertical datum (measured from the geoid). Key parameters include a vertical shift (an added constant), direction (+1 for heights, −1 for depths), and a linear unit. VCS (ellipsoidal) options are common; see Esri’s documentation for the most used vertical systems. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Transformations

Why transform? The dashed black line in many diagrams represents a theoretical ellipsoid that may not match the earth’s surface at your location. You might start with a global ellipsoid/datum (e.g., WGS84) and need to move to a local datum (e.g., NAD83) that better fits your area. A transformation shifts/rotates/scales coordinates from one GCS to another (e.g., WGS84 → NAD83). Think of the ellipsoid as the size/shape, and the datum as what fixes that ellipsoid to the earth. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Example: If your orthoimagery uses “NAD 1983 State Plane Maine West FIPS 1802” and your point data are in “NAD 1927 State Plane Maine West FIPS 1802”, they won’t align until you apply a datum transformation (e.g., “NAD 1927 to NAD 1983 NADCON”). Datum transformations typically move features by up to ~100 meters—imperceptible at small scales but crucial as you zoom in. One workflow georeferences right-of-way plans using northings/eastings for better accuracy; point locations originally in WKID 26784 (NAD 1927) were projected to WKID 26848 (NAD 1983), involving a transformation from D_North_American_1927 (Clarke 1866) to D_North_American_1983 (GRS 1980). :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Two kinds of transformations exist: geographic (horizontal) between GCSs, and vertical between vertical datums (ellipsoidal vs. geoidal). Depending on your data, you may need both. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}
